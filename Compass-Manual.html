Welcome & Overview

What is Compass?

Compass is a comprehensive, year-long college application operating system designed specifically for Grade 11-12 students applying to universities worldwide. It helps you organize, track, and optimize every aspect of your university application journey—from managing multiple school applications to building a compelling extracurricular profile, achieving target test scores, and crafting compelling essays.

💡 Think of it as your personal application command center: One place to manage schools, activities, tests, essays, and milestones across a 24-month timeline.

Who is it for?

Compass is built for:

  • Grade 11-12 students applying to universities internationally
  • Students targeting US, UK, Canada, Australia, Hong Kong, or Singapore universities
  • Students navigating multiple application systems (Common App, UCAS, etc.)
  • Students balancing academics, tests, activities, and essays

Key Features Overview

🏫 Schools

Manage applications to multiple universities with tier tracking (Reach, Match, Safe) and intelligent requirements engine.

🎯 Activities

Track extracurriculars and super-curriculars with tier system and impact statements.

📊 Tests

Log test scores (TOEFL, IELTS, SAT, ACT, IB, A-Level, AP) with automatic normalization.

📋 Roadmap

17 milestones spanning Grade 11 to post-enrollment tracking.

✍️ Essays

Write, revise, and get AI feedback on personal statements and supplementals.

🤖 AI Coaching

Gap detection, monthly check-ins, and personalized guidance.

Supported Countries

Primary targets: United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia

Additional support: Hong Kong, Singapore

⚠️ Note: Compass is optimized for English-medium education systems. Some features (like certain milestone types) are region-specific.

Schools

Overview

Understanding Tiers

REACH Schools

Universities where your academic profile falls below their typical admitted student range. Reaching requires excellent overall application strength, unique activities, and strong essays.

Typical profile: Admitted students score in the 90th+ percentile. You need Tier 1 activities, strong essays, and above-average test scores.

MATCH Schools

Universities where your academic profile is in or near their typical admitted student range. Match schools are your "target" schools where you're likely to be admitted with a strong application.

Typical profile: Your test scores and GPA are in the 25th-75th percentile of admitted students.

SAFE Schools

Universities where your academic profile exceeds their typical admitted student range. Safe schools are where you're very likely to be admitted (assuming a submitted application).

Typical profile: Your test scores and GPA are above the 75th percentile.

💡 School list strategy: Aim for 70% Match, 20% Safe, 10% Reach schools. This maximizes your chances of admission to excellent universities.

Activities

Activity Tier System

Compass categorizes extracurriculars into three tiers to help you build a competitive profile.

🏆 Tier 1 Activities

Level: National or international achievement

Examples:

  • National science olympiad winner
  • International debate champion
  • Published research in peer-reviewed journal
  • National sports ranking (e.g., top 100 in country)
  • Award from government or major organization

Contribution to Readiness Score: 5 points each (max 2 = 10 points)

Why it matters: Tier 1 activities show exceptional achievement and are critical for Reach schools.

💼 Tier 2 Activities

Level: Regional achievement or school/community leadership

Examples:

  • Debate team captain or award winner (state/regional)
  • Volunteer coordinator (50+ hours/year)
  • Start-up founder or business leader
  • Sports team captain or competitive athlete
  • School club president (with measurable impact)

Contribution to Readiness Score: 2 points each (max 4 = 8 points)

Why it matters: Tier 2 shows leadership and commitment. Most Match schools expect at least 2-3.

📚 Tier 3 Activities

Level: Participation-based (no leadership or major award required)

Examples:

  • School club membership
  • Community volunteer (under 50 hours/year)
  • School sports team member (not captain)
  • Online course (e.g., Coursera, MIT OpenCourseWare)

Contribution to Readiness Score: 0.5 points each (max 10 = 5 points)

Why it matters: Breadth of activities shows diverse interests.

Adding an Activity: Step-by-Step

  • Activity Name: Official name (e.g., "Science Olympiad" not "science club")
  • Category: Extracurricular, Super-Curricular, Award, Research
  • Start Date & End Date: When you participated
  • Tier: Select 1, 2, or 3 based on level of achievement
  • Your Role: Participant, team member, captain, founder, organizer, etc.
  • Hours per Week: Average hours committed (used for impact calculation)

Impact Statement (max 150 characters): A short explanation of why this activity matters to you and what you gained.

Good example: "Led team to state competition. Developed leadership skills and discovered passion for STEM education."

Poor example: "I did the activity and it was good."

💡 Impact statement tips:
  • Show tangible results ("led" not "was part of")
  • Connect to your major or post-grad goals if possible
  • Be specific (what skills did you develop?)

Activity Categories

Category Definition Examples
Extracurricular (EC) Activities outside regular school curriculum Sports, clubs, student government
Super-Curricular Activities that deeply extend school curriculum (UK-specific bonus: +2 pts) Research project, independent study, MOOC in your subject
Award Recognitions and honors Scholarship, grant, essay award, athlete of year
Research Academic research or independent investigation Published paper, summer research, lab work
⚠️ UK Super-Curricular Bonus: If you're applying to UK universities, Compass awards +2 bonus points for super-curricular activities. This reflects the UK's emphasis on demonstrated subject knowledge and independent learning. Plan Tier 2-3 super-curriculars in your Grade 11 Spring/Summer.

Tips for Building a Strong EC Profile

💡 Depth over breadth: 2-3 activities with deep commitment beats 10 activities with shallow involvement. Aim for 100+ hours/year in at least one activity.
💡 Leadership progression: Start as participant → take on leadership roles in Grade 12. Show growth and commitment.
💡 Alignment to major: If possible, connect activities to your intended major (computer science students: coding competitions; pre-meds: volunteer healthcare).
💡 One "spike": Reach schools want one Tier 1 activity or one area of deep expertise. This is more compelling than being "well-rounded" with no distinction.

Score Normalization

Compass converts all test scores to a unified 0-20 scale for fair comparison. Here's how:

Language Tests (0-20 pts)

  • TOEFL: Score ÷ 120 × 20 (so 120 = 20, 60 = 10)
  • IELTS: Band ÷ 9 × 20 (so 9.0 = 20, 6.0 = 13.3)

Academic Tests (0-20 pts)

  • SAT: (Score - 400) ÷ 1200 × 20 (so 1600 = 20, 1000 = 10)
  • ACT: (Score ÷ 36) × 20 (so 36 = 20, 18 = 10)
  • IB: (Score ÷ 45) × 20 (so 45 = 20, 22.5 = 10)
  • A-Level: Based on A*, A, B, C grades (A* = 20, A = 18, B = 16, etc.) for each subject, averaged
  • AP: (Average score across all AP exams ÷ 5) × 20 (so avg 5 = 20, avg 2.5 = 10)
💡 Why normalization matters: SAT 1500 and IB 42 aren't directly comparable, but both might normalize to ~19/20. This helps Compass fairly rank your academic readiness across different systems.

Target Scores

Based on your school list tier distribution, Compass suggests target scores:

  • Language target: Based on Reach schools' typical TOEFL/IELTS requirements (e.g., 100+ TOEFL)
  • Academic target: Weighted by tier (Reach, Match, Safe averages)

In the Tests section, click "Set Target" for any test. Compass will suggest a score based on your school list, or you can enter a custom target. Your Readiness score will show progress toward target.

Retake Strategy

🤖 AI Retake Recommendation: After you receive a score, Compass AI analyzes your progress and recommends whether to retake. For example: "Your TOEFL is 88/120 (target 100). Statistically, students improve 10-15 points on second attempts. Consider retaking in 4 weeks."
💡 Retake timing:
  • Give yourself 2-4 weeks between attempts
  • Plan retakes for Grade 11 Spring (tests) and Grade 12 Early Fall (final attempts)
  • SAT: Space attempts 3 weeks apart minimum
  • TOEFL/IELTS: Space 12 days apart minimum (officially), but practically 2-4 weeks recommended

Roadmap & Milestones

Two-Year Application Arc

Compass lays out 17 critical milestones across a 24-month timeline (Grade 11 Fall → Grade 12 Late Spring). This roadmap ensures you're on track throughout your application journey.

Grade 11: Foundation Building

Theme: Research & Profile Building

Milestone 1: Research Top 10 Universities (Target: Dec 1) — Identify potential schools across tiers. Create a list of 15-20 schools to explore.
Milestone 2: Attend College Fair (if available) (Target: Oct-Nov) — Meet admissions officers, learn about schools, get on mailing lists.
Milestone 3: Identify Recommenders (Target: Nov 1) — Ask 2-3 teachers if they'd write recommendations (do this in person, not email). Typically: 2 academic teachers + 1 counselor.
Milestone 4: EC Deep Dive (Target: Dec 1) — Commit to deepen 2-3 activities. Take on leadership role (team captain, club officer, volunteer coordinator).

Theme: Test Preparation & School List Finalization

Milestone 5: Finalize School List (Draft) (Target: Jan 15) — Narrow to 15-20 schools. Establish tier distribution.
Milestone 6: Register for SAT/ACT (Target: Feb 1) — Register for spring test (March, May, or June). Aim for first attempt in spring.
Milestone 7: Language Test Planning (Target: Feb 1, if needed) — Register for TOEFL/IELTS if English isn't native language. Typically May/June.
Milestone 8: First Test Attempt (Target: Mar-Jun) — Take SAT or ACT. Plan to retake if needed.
Milestone 9: Summer Plan (Target: Apr 1) — Plan summer activities: test retakes, summer programs, research, internships, volunteering.

Grade 12: Application Season

Theme: Setup & Test Finalization

Milestone 10: Set Up Compass (Target: Sep 1) — Create account, input all 15-20 schools, activities, tests.
Milestone 11: Finalize Test Scores (Target: Sep 15) — Last retake window. Lock in your best scores (SAT by mid-Sep, language test by late Sep).
Milestone 12: Submit Recommender Requests (Target: Sep 20) — Formally request recommendations. Provide deadline (typically early Jan for US) and brief summary of your app.

Theme: Early Applications & Writing

Milestone 13: Early Decision Submission (if applicable) (Target: Nov 1-15) — If applying Early Decision/Action, submit by deadline (binding or non-binding).
Milestone 14: UCAS Submission (UK students) (Target: Oct 15 for early birds, Oct 31 typical) — Submit UCAS application with personal statement.
Milestone 15: Personal Statement Final Draft (Target: Oct 31) — Complete personal statement (650 words Common App or 4000 chars UCAS). Share with teachers/counselor for feedback.
Milestone 16: School-Specific Essays Start (Target: Nov 1) — Begin "Why School?" and supplemental essays for Regular Decision schools.

Theme: Regular Decision Submissions & Interviews

Milestone 17: Regular Decision Submissions Complete (Target: Jan 31) — Submit all remaining applications before deadlines (typical window: Jan 1-Feb 1).
Milestone 18: Interviews (if requested) (Target: Jan-Feb) — Attend alumni or school interviews for schools that offer them.
Milestone 19: Financial Aid Forms (FAFSA/CSS) (Target: Jan-Feb) — Submit US financial aid forms. Check Canada/UK/AU equivalents.

Theme: Decisions & Enrollment

Milestone 20: Decisions Received (Target: Mar-Apr) — Schools release decisions (US: end of March; UK: Jan-May depending on offer conditions).
Milestone 21: Compare & Choose University (Target: Apr-May) — Compare offers, financial aid, consider campus visits. Make final enrollment decision by May 1 (US) or May 31 (most others).
Milestone 22: Accept Offer & Enroll (Target: May 31) — Confirm enrollment by deadline. Pay enrollment deposit.
Milestone 23: Official Transcript Submit (Target: May-Jun) — Send official Grade 12 transcript to enrolled university.
Milestone 24: Visa/Housing Plans (International) (Target: Jun-Jul) — If international: apply for visa, book accommodation, arrange travel.

HIGH

MEDIUM

  • LOW gaps can wait unless they're quick (e.g., adding impact statement)

Essay Workshop

Essay Types

Compass supports two main essay types:

Personal Statement (650 words, all schools)

  • Purpose: Give admissions officers insight into who you are beyond grades and test scores
  • Platforms: Common App (650 words) for US/CA, UCAS (4000 characters) for UK
  • Tone: Personal, reflective, authentic voice
  • Topics: Identity, background, challenge overcome, moment that shaped you, intellectual interest, etc.

Supplemental Essays (250-500 words each, school-specific)

  • Purpose: Show why you're interested in this specific school (fit)
  • Types: "Why [School]?", activities, challenges, intellectual curiosity, demographics, etc.
  • Tone: Research-informed, specific to school (not generic)
  • Typical count: 1-3 supplementals per school (Reach schools have more)

Adding a New Essay

  • Personal Statement: If you have only one, all schools see it
  • Supplemental/Why School: Linked to a specific school (e.g., "Why MIT?")
  • Other: Select activity, challenge, intellectual interest, etc.

Copy the exact essay prompt from the school (if it's a supplemental). If personal statement, you can use Common App prompt or write a custom prompt.

Start writing or paste existing draft. Compass auto-saves as you type.

Drafts System (Versioning)

Compass saves every version of your essay with timestamps. You can:

  • Create new draft: Click "New Draft" to start revising without losing previous version
  • View history: See all draft versions with dates (e.g., "Draft 1 - Sep 15", "Draft 2 - Sep 22")
  • Restore old draft: If you over-edit, restore any previous version
  • Compare versions: See what changed between drafts

Writing & Revising Workflow

Before writing, brainstorm your essay. Some prompts to guide you:

For Personal Statements:

  • What moment or experience has shaped who you are?
  • What's an obstacle you've overcome?
  • What's a core part of your identity (background, culture, beliefs)?
  • What intellectual curiosity drives you?

For Supplementals:

  • Why this school? (Be specific: research, programs, culture, values alignment)
  • How does your activity connect to the school's offerings?
  • What would you contribute to campus?

Write your essay without worrying about perfection. Goals:

  • Get ideas on paper
  • Hit word count (aim for 80% on first draft, e.g., 500/650)
  • Use specific examples and stories (not general statements)
💡 First draft tips:
  • Write without editing. Turn off your inner critic.
  • Use vivid details and specific moments (show, don't tell)
  • Write in your authentic voice (imagine telling a friend)

Share your draft with teachers, counselors, or mentors. Request feedback on:

  • Is my voice authentic?
  • Are my examples specific and vivid?
  • Does the essay answer the prompt clearly?
  • Is there anything that feels clichéd or generic?
⚠️ Keep feedback focused: Aim for 1-2 trusted readers, not 5+. Too many opinions will confuse your voice.

In Compass, create new drafts as you revise. Focus on:

  • Structure: Does the essay flow logically?
  • Clarity: Are ideas clearly expressed?
  • Conciseness: Cut redundancy and weak words
  • Grammar & mechanics: Final proofread
  • Word count: Hit the exact limit (650 Common App, 4000 chars UCAS)

Once finalized:

  • Mark draft as "Final"
  • Copy & paste into application platform (Common App, UCAS, school portals)
  • Double-check formatting is preserved
  • Submit application

2. Clarity & Structure Analysis

Analyzes essay organization and clarity. Identifies:

  • Unclear transitions between paragraphs
  • Paragraphs that lack a clear topic sentence
  • Ideas that feel disconnected or abrupt

3. Personal Voice Authenticity

Assesses whether the essay sounds like you (authentic) or overly polished/inauthentic. Looks for:

  • Consistent tone and perspective
  • Personal anecdotes and specific details
  • Language that feels natural (not over-thesaurus'd)

4. Word Count Compliance

Checks if you're within limits. Shows: "650/650 words. Perfect. (If 630-670, you're fine; under 600 or over 700 = need revision)"

5. School-Specific Fit (Supplementals Only)

For "Why School?" essays, analyzes if you've demonstrated genuine fit. Checks for:

  • Specific mention of programs, professors, resources (not generic)
  • Personal connection explained (why this school for YOU?)
  • Alignment with your academic/personal goals

Example feedback: "You mention MIT's CS program but don't explain why MIT specifically fits your goals. Add: How MIT's X program differs from others, and why that difference matters to you."

Feedback Tips

💡 Using AI feedback:
  • AI feedback is a starting point, not a prescription. You choose what to act on.
  • Don't over-polish. Admissions officers prefer authentic, personal essays over perfectly "polished" ones.
  • If AI flags something but your human reader says it's great, trust the human.

Monthly Check-in

Overview

The Monthly Check-in is a structured reflection (takes ~10 minutes) that Compass uses to generate personalized AI coaching. Complete one per month during application season.

5 Structured Questions

Requirement: Answer minimum 3 of 5 questions (you can answer all 5 for more detailed coaching).

"What have you accomplished this month (tests, essays, schools added, activities, milestones)?"

Be specific: "Submitted SAT (1420), drafted personal statement (300 words), added 5 schools to Compass, completed impact statements for 3 activities."

"What difficulties or blockers did you face? What's slowing you down?"

Examples: Test anxiety, essay writer's block, unsure about school choices, struggling to balance with schoolwork.

"Do you need to improve any test scores? Which tests, and by how much?"

Example: "TOEFL 82, need 95 for Reach schools (+13 points). Retaking in March."

"What's your essay status? Which essays are done, which need work?"

Example: "Personal statement draft 2 (500 words), need to expand. MIT supplemental started (150 words). Stanford Why essay not started."

"What's your biggest worry or concern about the application process right now?"

Be honest. Compass AI responds with compassionate, realistic guidance.

AI Coaching Summary

After you submit your check-in, Compass AI generates a personalized coaching response (200-260 words) addressing:

  • Validation of your accomplishments (celebrate wins)
  • Advice on blockers (actionable solutions)
  • Test retake strategy (if needed)
  • Essay timeline & priorities
  • Encouragement & perspective on your biggest worry
🤖 AI Coaching Example: "You've made great progress (SAT +50 points, personal statement underway). Your TOEFL gap is real but manageable—focus on listening practice. For essays: finish personal statement by Oct 15, then tackle supplementals. Your worry about Reach schools is valid, but your profile (Tier 1 activity + improving scores) is competitive. Keep moving forward."

Bilingual Output

You can request your coaching summary in English or Chinese (中文). Select your language before submitting, and Compass AI will respond in that language.

Frequency & Timing

  • When to do check-ins: Once per month (recommend: first Sunday of the month)
  • When to start: Grade 12 September (when you set up Compass)
  • When to end: After you enroll (Milestone 22)
  • Total check-ins: Typically 8-9 over application season
💡 Check-in strategy: Schedule a recurring reminder on your phone for the same day/time each month (e.g., first Sunday, 10 AM). Consistency helps Compass track your progress accurately.

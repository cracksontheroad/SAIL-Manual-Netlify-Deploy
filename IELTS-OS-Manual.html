IELTS Test Overview

The IELTS exam tests English proficiency across four skills, each with specific formats and scoring:

Skill Duration Score Range Key Components
Listening ~30 min 0-40 (scaled to 1-9) 4 sections, 40 questions, audio playback once
Reading 60 min 0-40 (scaled to 1-9) 3 academic passages, 40 questions, multiple question types
Writing 60 min Band 1-9 Task 1 (20 min): 150+ words; Task 2 (40 min): 250+ words
Speaking 11-14 min Band 1-9 Interview with examiner: Part 1 (4 min), Part 2 (2 min), Part 3 (5 min)

Band Score Scale (1.0-9.0)

IELTS scores range from 1.0 (non-user) to 9.0 (expert user). Here's what each band represents:

Band Description Typical Characteristics
9.0 Expert User Fluent, accurate, sophisticated English. Rare for non-natives.
8.0-8.5 Very Good User Fluent with occasional errors. Strong vocabulary and grammar.
7.0-7.5 Good User Generally fluent. Some errors in complex structures. Acceptable for most universities.
6.0-6.5 Competent User Adequate performance. Some errors but generally understood. Common target.
5.0-5.5 Modest User Manages mostly simple language. Frequent errors in complex structures.
4.0-4.5 Limited User Basic communication. Limited vocabulary and grammatical range.
1.0-3.5 Non/Minimal User Minimal English ability. Significant communication barriers.

Writing Studio

Overview

Writing Studio is where you practice both IELTS Task 1 (descriptive writing: 150+ words, 20 minutes) and Task 2 (argumentative/discursive essay: 250+ words, 40 minutes). Each submission receives instant AI scoring and detailed rubric feedback.

AI Scoring: Your essays are scored using Claude AI aligned with official IELTS Writing rubric criteria: Task Achievement/Response, Coherence & Cohesion, Lexical Resource, and Grammatical Range & Accuracy.

  2. Set Writing Time: Choose a timer (20 min for Task 1, 40 min for Task 2, or custom). The timer starts when you begin typing and can be paused.
  3. Compose Your Essay: Use the rich-text editor to write. Features include:

  7. Rewrite and Resubmit: Make improvements based on feedback and submit again. Each submission is tracked in your history.

IELTS Writing Rubric Explained

Your essays are scored on these 4 criteria, each equally weighted (25% each):

What it measures: How well you address the task prompt and provide relevant information.

Band 7-9: Fully addresses the task with accurate, well-developed information. Task 2: takes clear position and supports with detailed examples. Task 1: describes all main features.

Band 5-6: Addresses most of the task. Information is mostly accurate but may lack development. Minor irrelevancies.

Band 4: Attempts the task but misses key aspects. Information may be inaccurate or underdeveloped.

Tips to improve: Reread the prompt twice before writing. Ensure every paragraph directly answers the question. Include specific examples.

What it measures: How logically organized your ideas are and how well sentences/paragraphs connect.

Band 7-9: Clear progression of ideas. Paragraphs are well-structured. Transitions between ideas are smooth (however, therefore, furthermore). Pronouns and referencing are clear.

Band 5-6: Ideas are organized but progression may be unclear. Some conjunctions and transitions present but not always accurate. Mostly clear references.

Band 4: Limited organization. Few transitional phrases. Some confusing references or pronoun errors.

Tips to improve: Use a clear structure: intro (1 para) → body (2-3 para, each with one main idea) → conclusion. Use topic sentences. Use transitional words (firstly, moreover, in conclusion, etc.). Check that "it" and "this" clearly refer to something.

What it measures: Your vocabulary range and accuracy of word choice.

Band 7-9: Wide vocabulary range. Uses less common words accurately. Shows awareness of synonyms and registers (formal/informal). Correct spelling and word forms.

Band 5-6: Adequate vocabulary for the task. Some variety but often repetitive. May use common phrases. Minor spelling/word-form errors.

Band 4: Limited vocabulary. Frequent repetition. Common errors in word choice or spelling.

Tips to improve: Learn collocations (common word pairs: "strong evidence," "significant increase"). Use synonyms to avoid repetition. Check word forms (noun, verb, adjective, adverb). Study academic vocabulary lists.

What it measures: Complexity of sentence structures and accuracy of grammar.

Band 7-9: Uses complex sentences with multiple clauses accurately. Various tenses used correctly. Rare errors that don't impede communication. Strong control of grammar.

Band 5-6: Mostly correct simple and some complex sentences. Generally accurate tenses but occasional errors in more complex structures. Most errors don't affect understanding.

Band 4: Simple sentences mostly correct. Frequent errors in tense, subject-verb agreement, or complex structures. Errors sometimes obstruct meaning.

Tips to improve: Practice complex sentences with dependent clauses (using "although," "because," "which," etc.). Review tense usage: simple present for facts, present perfect for recent actions, conditionals for hypotheticals. Proofread for subject-verb agreement and prepositions.

Task 1 vs Task 2 Differences

Aspect Task 1 (Academic) Task 2
Time Limit 20 minutes 40 minutes
Word Count Minimum 150 words Minimum 250 words
Task Type Describe a visual: chart, graph, table, diagram, map, or process Write an essay: Agree/Disagree, Discuss Both Views, Problem/Solution, or Opinion
Style Formal, objective, factual Formal, argumentative, personal stance
Focus Describe accurately; highlight key features Persuade with arguments; support with examples
Pro Tip: Task 1 is worth 33% of Writing band; Task 2 is 67%. However, many test-takers rush Task 1 to finish Task 2. Instead, allocate time: spend 20 min on Task 1 (including planning), 40 min on Task 2 (including planning and checking).

Tips for Improving Your Writing Score

  • Plan Before Writing: Spend 2-3 minutes noting your main ideas and structure before you start. This prevents rambling.
  • Write in Paragraphs: Each paragraph should have one main idea. Use topic sentences and supporting sentences.
  • Vary Sentence Length: Mix short punchy sentences with longer complex ones. This shows grammatical range.
  • Check Word Counts: Task 1 minimum 150 words, Task 2 minimum 250 words. Going under means penalty.
  • Avoid Casual Language: IELTS Writing requires formal register. Replace "kids" with "children," "cause" (verb) with "result in," etc.
  • Use Passive Voice Sparingly: Task 1 (graphs/charts) may need passive voice. Task 2: overuse of passive voice suggests limited grammatical range.
  • Practice Tense Consistency: Task 1 uses simple present (graph shows...). Task 2: review your tense choices (past for examples, present for general truths).
  • Proofread (Last 2-3 min): Check for typos, missing articles (a, the), subject-verb agreement, prepositions.

Reading Practice

Overview

IELTS Reading requires you to answer 40 questions across three academic passages within 60 minutes. IELTS-OS provides practice sets at multiple difficulty levels with instant feedback.

Question Types Explained

Format: Question with 4 options (A, B, C, D). Select one correct answer.

Skill tested: Understanding main ideas, details, and implications.

Strategy: Read the question carefully. Skim the passage for key terms from the question. Reread that section. Eliminate obviously wrong answers first.

Format: Statement about the passage. Choose True (directly states), False (contradicts), or Not Given (not mentioned).

Skill tested: Distinguishing between information in the passage, opposite information, and absent information.

Strategy: "Not Given" is different from "False." If the passage doesn't mention something, it's Not Given (not False). Look for exact or paraphrased matches in the passage.

Format: List of headings (usually more than needed). Match each paragraph to its best heading.

Skill tested: Identifying main ideas of paragraphs.

Strategy: Read each paragraph quickly and note its main idea. Find the heading that best summarizes it (not just a detail). Headings are often paraphrased, not word-for-word.

Format: Incomplete sentence. Complete it using words from the passage (word limit given: usually 1-3 words).

Skill tested: Locating and extracting specific information.

Strategy: Underline the key terms in the incomplete sentence. Find them in the passage. Copy the exact words that fit (including spelling and grammar).

Format: Question that requires a brief answer in 1-3 words from the passage.

Skill tested: Locating and extracting specific factual information.

Strategy: Similar to sentence completion. Find the relevant sentence in the passage and extract the answer word-for-word.

Step-by-Step Reading Practice Walkthrough

  1. Select a Passage Set: Click "New Reading Test" and choose difficulty (Band 5-6, 6-7, 7-8, 8-9). You can also filter by question type mix.
  2. Start the Timed Section: Click "Begin Test." A 60-minute timer starts. You'll see all 3 passages with 40 questions total.
  3. Read First Passage & Answer Questions: Read passage 1 (typically 700-800 words). Then answer questions 1-13 (approximately). You can scroll between the passage and questions.
  4. Move Through Passages 2 & 3: Repeat for passages 2 and 3. Manage your time: aim for ~20 minutes per passage.
  5. Submit Answers: Click "Submit Test" when finished or time expires. System automatically grades all answers.
  6. View Immediate Feedback: You'll see:
    • Marked answer sheet (correct/incorrect)
    • Correct answers highlighted
    • Explanations for each question
    • Section score out of 40
    • Converted band score (0-40 scales to Band 1-9)
  7. Review Explanations: Click any question to see detailed explanation. Understand why incorrect answers were wrong.

Reading Strategies & Tips

  • Skim Before Deep Reading: Quickly read the first sentence of each paragraph to understand the passage structure. Then tackle questions.
  • Underline Key Terms in Questions: Before searching the passage, note important words. This speeds up location finding.
  • Match Paraphrases: The passage rarely uses exact question wording. Recognize synonyms and rephrasing.
  • Avoid Over-Inference: Base answers strictly on the passage. Don't use outside knowledge, even if you think it's correct.
  • Manage Time Carefully: If stuck on a question, mark it and move on. Come back if you have time. 40 questions in 60 min ≈ 1.5 min per question.
  • Use Passage Numbers: Questions usually follow passage order. Passage 1 questions appear first, then passage 2, etc. Use this to predict where to search.

Time Management for Reading

Recommended Time Allocation:

  • Passage 1 (minutes 0-20): Skim (2 min) → Answer questions (18 min)
  • Passage 2 (minutes 20-40): Skim (2 min) → Answer questions (18 min)
  • Passage 3 (minutes 40-55): Skim (2 min) → Answer questions (13 min)
  • Buffer (minutes 55-60): Review flagged questions, check answers

Listening Practice

Overview

IELTS Listening has 4 sections with different themes and question types. You hear each section once (no replay), so focus is critical. IELTS-OS provides unlimited practice with transcript access and instant marking.

The 4 Listening Sections

Format: Conversation between 2 people in everyday situations (e.g., booking accommodation, inquiring about a course, making a complaint).

Length: ~3 minutes

Focus: Specific information, details, spelling, numbers.

Question types: Form completion, note-taking, multiple choice

Tip: Listen for keywords and speaker's purpose. The first speaker sets context; the second asks questions or provides information.

Format: One speaker (e.g., tour guide, event organizer, announcer) presenting information to a group.

Length: ~3 minutes

Focus: Main information, supporting details, descriptions.

Question types: Matching, sentence completion, multiple choice, plan/map labeling

Tip: Predict what information will come (dates, prices, locations). Focus on transitions (the next thing..., before that...).

Format: Conversation between 2-3 people (typically student and tutor) discussing an academic topic or project.

Length: ~3-4 minutes

Focus: Understanding discussion flow, opinions, conclusions, academic vocabulary.

Question types: Multiple choice, matching, form/table completion

Tip: Recognize disagreements and changes of mind ("Actually, I think..."). Note academic terms and how they're used.

Format: One speaker delivering an academic lecture on a topic in science, history, geography, etc.

Length: ~3-4 minutes

Focus: Main ideas, explanations, examples, definitions.

Question types: Sentence completion, note-taking, summary completion, classification

Tip: Listen for signposts ("There are three main causes..."). Predict what comes after ("Let's look at the first one...").

Common Question Types

  • Multiple Choice: Choose the best answer from A, B, C, or D.
  • Short Answer: Answer in 1-3 words (from the audio, not your knowledge).
  • Form Completion: Fill in a form with information you hear (names, numbers, dates, prices).
  • Sentence Completion: Complete sentences using words from the audio (word limit usually 1-3 words).
  • Matching: Match ideas, people, or activities to descriptions from the audio.
  • Summary/Classification: Classify statements or summarize information heard.

Audio Controls & Features

When you start a listening test, you'll have these controls:

  • Play/Pause Button: Start and pause the audio (you control when to listen).
  • Progress Bar: Shows where you are in the section.
  • Speed Control: Slow down to 0.8x or 0.9x if needed (not available on real exam, so use sparingly).
  • Volume: Adjust volume as needed.
  • Skip to Section: Jump directly to Section 2, 3, or 4 (after Section 1 is answered).
  • Question Navigator: See all questions and click to jump between them.

After Completion: Transcripts & Marking

Once you submit your listening test:

  1. Your answers are instantly marked (correct/incorrect).
  2. You can toggle on the full audio transcript to read what you heard.
  3. Correct answers are highlighted; explanations provided for wrong answers.
  4. Your raw score (out of 40) is converted to IELTS Band (1-9).
  5. Breakdown by section shows which sections need improvement.

Listening Tips & Strategies

  • Read Questions First: Before audio plays, read questions and underline key words. This preps your mind for what to listen for.
  • Predict Answers: Based on the question and context, predict what kind of answer to expect (a name, a number, a reason).
  • Write Quickly: Don't worry about grammar or spelling during the test. Write keywords. You can refine later.
  • Don't Pause Repeatedly: Try to listen in natural flow. Constant pausing disrupts comprehension.
  • Check Spelling & Grammar: In the 10-minute transfer time (real exam), check your written answers for spelling and grammar. This can prevent "wrong" answers due to typos.
  • Listen for Synonyms: The audio rarely uses exact question wording. Listen for similar meanings and paraphrasing.
  • Note Numbers Carefully: Listening tests often have numbers (prices, quantities, years). Listen very carefully to these.

Vocabulary Drills

Overview

Vocabulary is essential for IELTS success. IELTS-OS uses spaced repetition (showing words at optimal intervals) to ensure long-term retention. Vocabulary is organized by band level to match your goals.

Practice Modes

A word is shown with 4 possible definitions. Select the correct one.

Skill: Understanding word meaning.

Strategy: If you're unsure, eliminate obviously wrong definitions. Think about word families and roots.

A sentence with a blank appears. Select the correct word to complete it (from 4 options).

Skill: Understanding word usage in context.

Strategy: Read the full sentence for context clues. Even if you don't know the word, context may help.

Given a word (e.g., "contribute"), generate its word family (contributes, contributor, contribution, contributory). You type the variants.

Skill: Understanding how words change form (noun, verb, adjective, adverb).

Strategy: Know common suffixes: -tion (noun), -ent/-ing (adjective), -ly (adverb), -able (adjective).

A short passage is given with a blank. Choose the word that best fits (from 4 options).

Skill: Using broader context to understand word usage.

Strategy: Read the full passage, not just the sentence with the blank. Context helps disambiguate.

Band-Level Vocabulary Tiers

Band Level Word Count Target Focus
Band 5-6 2,000 common words Everyday vocabulary, basic academic words, common phrasal verbs
Band 6-7 2,500 words Less common words, more phrasal verbs, topic-specific vocabulary
Band 7-8 3,000+ words Sophisticated vocabulary, synonyms, nuanced word choices, advanced collocations
Band 8-9 3,500+ words Rare and specialized vocabulary, deep word knowledge, precise word selection

Academic Collocations

Collocations are words that frequently appear together (e.g., "strong evidence," "significant increase"). IELTS-OS includes a dedicated collocation module for each band level.

Tip: Learning collocations is more valuable than isolated words. In writing, using correct collocations shows advanced vocabulary knowledge and earns higher scores.

Study Plans & Tips

Recommended Weekly Schedule

For students targeting 1.0 band improvement, here's a balanced weekly schedule:

Day Focus Area Duration Activity
Monday Writing 60 min Task 2 essay (40 min writing, 20 min review)
Tuesday Vocabulary 45 min Drill target band word list (definition matching + sentence completion)
Wednesday Reading 70 min Full reading test (60 min + 10 min review)
Thursday Listening 60 min Full listening test (30 min + 10 min transcript review + 20 min error analysis)
Friday Writing 60 min Task 1 practice (20 min writing, 40 min review and rewrite)
Saturday Comprehensive 90 min Full mock test (all 4 skills) or focus on weakest area
Sunday Review 30-45 min Vocabulary drills or review past feedback; light practice

Total weekly commitment: ~375-420 minutes (6-7 hours). Adjust based on your baseline and goals.

Skill Balance Strategy

Different skills require different approaches. Here's how to allocate your effort:

Best for: Beginners (Band 4-5) who need to improve all skills equally.

Allocation: 25% Writing, 25% Reading, 25% Listening, 25% Vocabulary.

Rationale: Building a solid foundation across all areas prevents weak spots.

Best for: Intermediate students (Band 6-7) with one notably weak skill.

Example allocation: If Reading is 1.5 bands below your other skills: 20% Writing, 40% Reading, 20% Listening, 20% Vocabulary.

Rationale: Intensive focus on your weakest skill brings up the overall band faster.

Best for: Very limited time; learners who prefer deep focus.

Example: Week 1-3 focus on Writing, Week 4-6 focus on Reading, etc.

Rationale: Mastering one skill fully can boost confidence and momentum. However, this approach risks neglecting other skills, so include maintenance sessions.

Common Mistakes by Band Level

  • Writing: Not understanding the task (writing irrelevant content), using too much informal language, major grammar errors that obscure meaning.
  • Reading: Not recognizing paraphrasing; looking for exact keyword matches; not finishing all 40 questions due to slow reading.
  • Listening: Anxiety causing you to miss the answer while worrying about previous questions; not reading questions first.
  • Vocabulary: Trying to memorize word lists without context; not learning word families and collocations.
  • Writing: Unclear paragraph organization; repetitive vocabulary despite knowing better words; over-reliance on templates; minor grammar errors not caught in proofreading.
  • Reading: Careless errors (misreading a key word); rushing on easy sections; not using time management effectively; confusion between "Not Given" and "False."
  • Listening: Missing details due to focusing only on main ideas; spelling errors on proper nouns or numbers; difficulty with British/American accent variation.
  • Vocabulary: Knowing the word but choosing wrong definition based on first letter or partial memory; not reviewing collocation mistakes.
  • Writing: Over-complicating sentences; subtle grammar errors (article misuse in complex contexts); less common spelling mistakes; minor issues with register or formality.
  • Reading: Tricky multiple-choice questions where 2-3 answers seem correct; missing nuanced meanings or implications; time pressure causing rushed final questions.
  • Listening: Advanced speakers using rapid speech or heavy accents; understanding individual words but not grasping the intended meaning; overconfidence leading to careless mistakes.
  • Vocabulary: Confusing near-synonyms (effect vs affect); subtle collocation errors (strong opinion vs firm opinion); rare word recognition without deep context.

Time Management Exam Tips

  • Reading (60 min): Allocate 20 min per passage including reading and answering. Use the last 5-10 min to check answers and fill any blanks.
  • Writing (60 min): Allocate 20 min to Task 1 (including 2-3 min planning, 15 min writing, 2-3 min checking) and 40 min to Task 2 (including 3-5 min planning, 30-32 min writing, 2-3 min checking).
  • Listening (~30 min): You don't control the pace; audio plays once. Plan to listen carefully the first time. Use the 10-minute transfer time to check spelling.
  • Speaking (11-14 min): Part 1 (4 min): Answer personal questions naturally. Part 2 (2 min): Speak non-stop if possible. Part 3 (5-6 min): Discuss abstract ideas. No planning time for Parts 1 and 3, only Part 2 (1 minute to plan).

Writing Templates for Task 1 and Task 2

Recommended structure:

Paragraph 1 (Intro): "The chart/graph/diagram illustrates [main idea]. The data covers [time period/categories]. Key observations include [1-2 main points]."

Paragraph 2 (Main feature 1): Describe the most notable feature with specific numbers/percentages.

Paragraph 3 (Main feature 2): Describe the second most notable feature. Compare if relevant ("In contrast to... while...").

Paragraph 4 (Conclusion): "Overall, [summary of main trends]. This suggests [implication or significance]."

Word count: 150-180 words (not much more unless justified).

Recommended structure:

Paragraph 1 (Intro): Paraphrase the topic in 1-2 sentences. State your position/thesis in 1-2 sentences. ("While some argue X, I believe Y because...").

Paragraph 2 (Argument 1): Main argument with supporting evidence and example. Topic sentence → explanation → concrete example → concluding sentence linking back to thesis.

Paragraph 3 (Argument 2): Second argument, same structure. Consider counterargument and refutation if relevant.

Paragraph 4 (Argument 3, optional): If space/time allows, add a third argument or address counterargument.

Paragraph 5 (Conclusion): Restate thesis (paraphrased). Summarize main points. Add wider implication or recommendation.

Word count: 280-320 words. Avoid excessive length.

AI Transparency: Claude AI Scoring

IELTS-OS uses Claude AI (by Anthropic) for essay and writing assessment. Here's what you should know:

  • How It Works: Your essay is sent to Claude API with an IELTS rubric prompt. Claude evaluates your writing against each criterion and returns a score + feedback.
  • Data Privacy: Your essays are processed by Anthropic's Claude API but are not used to train Claude models. We don't share personal identifiers with Anthropic.
  • Accuracy: Claude's scoring correlates highly (R² > 0.85) with real IELTS examiners on a validation set. However, it's not 100% accurate. Treat AI scores as guidance, not final judgment.
  • Limitations: Claude may miss subtle errors, misinterpret intent, or score harshly on unconventional writing. Always review feedback critically.
  • Request Explanation: If you disagree with an AI score, you can request a detailed explanation of how each criterion was evaluated.
